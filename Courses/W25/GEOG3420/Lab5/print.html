Introduction

I assume that, at this point in the course, you have a good working knowledge of the Whitebox Workflows and general image processing techniques. Therefore, this final lab is more free-form than the previous assignments. You will be given some background information on what the general topics and requirements are for the lab, but it will be up to you as to how to carry out the analysis. One of the fundamental skills that you will need to take from this course is how to search for and find the information that you need to solve a remote-sensing problem.

In this assignment, you are asked to complete a land-use/land-cover change detection analysis for the nearly 10 years between August 19, 2014 and August 14, 2024. You have been provided with 8 bands of multispectral Landsat 8 imagery cropped to a section of Southern Ontario covering the Greater Toronto Area (GTA) for the two dates. Both image datasets are Level-2 (atmospherically corrected to surface reflectance/temperature) scenes (path 18, row 30). The band 10 images are different than the other Level-2 multispectral data in that it is emited (versus reflected) thermal infrared that has been atmospherically corrected to surface temperature data. These data are also originally relatively coarse 100m resolution but has been resampled to the same 30m resolution as the other multispectral imagery. There are also significant NoData gaps in these images that are the result of the atmospheric correction of thermal imagery. In addition to the Level-2 data, you have also been provided the Level-1 Band 8 (panchromatic band). This band is at finer spatial resolution (15m versus 30m) but has not been processed into surface reflectance. You will not be able to combine it with any other bands in analysis, but its finer spatial resolution may also be useful. Note that the image metadata, providing details of the image acquisition, is also distributed along with the imagery.

For this assignment, you are a remote sensing consultant, hired by me. Don't get too excited, I’m hiring you to work for free. I am an urban planner tasked with developing a plan for Ontario's growing population over the next decade. As such, I am interested in documenting and evaluating the urban expansion that has taken place in the GTA over the recent past, such that I may work with municipalities to plan for the anticipated population growth in their communities. Information about the previous development in the region will allow me to better characterize the capacity for growth in each community. Therefore, I am interested in documenting the recent development in the GTA and surrounding communities and have contracted you, a remote-sensing specialist working for a private consulting company, to aid with documenting urban expansion over the 10-year period between image acquisition dates. Note that this period has included some of the most rapid population growth in the Canada's history and many newcomers have chosen to settle in the GTA during this time span. I am particularly interested in documenting which areas of the region have experienced the greatest changes (urban expansion into surrounding rural areas) and documenting what type of urban development these past changes have been associated with (e.g. commercial/industrial growth, suburban development, etc.). My primary concern is to document expansion of residential neighbourhoods and commercial/industrial development. To answer these questions you will have to perform automated change detection analysis on the provided satellite imagery. It is up to you to research how to go about conducting this analysis but I have provided you with a short list of resources to help guide you. You should supplement this list with any additional resources on change detection analysis that you can find during your research, and of course, with the lecture slides on this topic.

Things to consider

As you begin your analysis you should note a few things about the data:

  1. The scenes are not perfectly cloud free. The original 2014 and 2024 images contain extensive areas of cloud cover but thankfully there was relatively little over the GTA, which is one reason why I have cropped the images. The presence of this cloud cover means that some apparent 'change' may be the difference between cloud/no-cloud between dates (and cloud shadow areas too). Be aware of this 'uninteresting change' as you interpret your results. The files LC08_L2SP_018030_20140819_20200911_02_T1_QA_PIXEL_clipped.TIF and LC08_L2SP_018030_20240814_20240822_02_T1_QA_PIXEL_clipped.TIF contain information about cloud and cloud shadow areas within each image. See LSDS-1619_Landsat8-9-Collection2-Level2-Science-Product-Guide-v5.pdf for descriptions of how to interpret the numeric values in these images.

  2. I've given you all of the data, but you don't necessarily need to use all of the available bands. Which bands will you choose to use? You can, of course, also use derived information products, such as vegetation indices (e.g., NDVI, EVI).

Change Detection Analysis Resources

Readings and background material

When you download the data for this lab exercise, notice that a sub-directory called Change detection resources is also created. In this folder, you will find a number of resources to help guide you through your change detection analysis.

Jensen, J.R. (2016) Introductory Digital Image Processing, Chapter 12. This is a set of lecture slides associated with Jensen's excellent digital image processing textbook. It provides quite detailed information and contains a wealth of background material on the topic of change detection analysis.

Mas, J.F. (1999) Monitoring land-cover changes: a comparison of change detection techniques, International Journal of Remote Sensing, 20(1): 139-152. This is an excellent journal article comparing some of the common techniques for performing change detection analysis. I'd certainly recommend reading this source carefully.

Mather, P.M. and Koch, M. (2011) Computer Processing of Remotely-Sensed Images. Chapter 6 Image Transforms, Section 6.8 Change Detection pp. 187-195.

And a short list of some other useful web resources:

Natural Resources Canada (NRCan) (2013) Land Use Change (Rural / Urban), online resource: https://www.nrcan.gc.ca/earth-sciences/geomatics/satellite-imagery-air-photos/satellite-imagery-products/educational-resources/9357.

Jennifer Pontius (2020) Change detection, online resource: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r7koPp0uJw8.

Whitebox Workflows support for change detection analysis

The Whitebox Workflows library contains several tools that can be useful in workflows for performing change detection analysis on two-date, multi-spectral satellite imagery. Some of the more common tools include, but are not limited to:

  • change_vector_analysis
  • cross_tabulation
  • image_regression
  • k_means_clustering
  • knn_classification
  • min_dist_classification
  • modified_k_means_clustering
  • normalized_difference_index
  • parallelepiped_classification
  • principal_component_analysis
  • random_forest_classification_fit
  • random_forest_classification_predict
  • svm_classification
  • write_function_memory_insertion

And, of course, there are the raster mathematical operations of image differencing (subtraction) and ratioing (division).

Please refer to the Whitebox Workflows for Python User Manual for details on the operation of each of the tools.

Grading the Final Report

The following grading scheme will be used to assess your report:

  • Introduction (5 marks)

    • A brief introduction to the problem.

  • Methods (20 marks)

    • Describe the data and the study site.
    • Description of the analysis approach.
    • Include the Python script that you used to help document your approach the the change detection analysis.
    • Be sure to use at least two different change detection approaches.
    • Describe the approaches that you used to address the problem. For example, what pre-processing techniques, if any, did you need use? Did you use pre- or post-classification approaches, or perhaps a combination of both? In other words, exactly what techniques did you select?
    • Be sure to provide justifications for the approaches that you chose.
    • What are some of the advantages and limitations of the approach that you used?

  • Results – Graphics (10 marks)

    • Results of analysis (images, maps, graphs, and/or tables where relevant).

  • Results – Documentation (20 marks)

    • Interpretation of results.
    • Statement of limitations of your results (i.e. because of limitations of your methodology).

  • General presentation (10 marks)

    • Neat and concise reporting
    • Spelling and grammar (be sure to proofread your work)
    • Organization (report is well laid-out using appropriate sections)
    • Consistent in-text citations and reference list.

The total is 65 marks.
